What Is Assault? 

Under Section 265 of the Criminal Code of Canada, assault is the intentional application of force to another person without their consent. This includes causing harm or even threatening violence.

Penalties for Assault

Penalties depend on the charge and its severity. Summary convictions can result in fines or shorter sentences, while indictable offences may lead to more severe penalties, including longer imprisonment. A criminal record could also impact your employment, travel, and personal relationships.

Common Defence Strategies for Assault Charges 

At Pyzer Criminal Lawyers, we explore all possible defence strategies based on the unique details of each case. Some of the most common defences include: 

Self-Defence

This defence argues that the accused used reasonable force to protect themselves or another person from harm. However, the force used must be proportionate to the threat faced. 

Lack of Intent 

An assault charge requires intent to apply force. If it can be shown that the accused did not intend to cause harmâ€”perhaps due to an accidentâ€”this can serve as a defence. 

Consent for the Use of Force 

In some cases, the alleged victim may have consented to the use of force, such as in a consensual fight or sporting event. If consent was given, this may negate the assault charge. 

Mistaken Belief in Consent 

This defence applies if the accused reasonably believed that the alleged victim had consented to the use of force. The circumstances must support this belief for it to be a valid defence. 

Reasonable Doubt 

In criminal cases, the prosecution must prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. If the evidence is insufficient or there are inconsistencies in witness testimony, a lawyer can argue that the case does not meet the burden of proof, leading to a dismissal of the charges or a possible acquittal.

Your Rights Under the Charter

As a Canadian citizen, you have rights under the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms that must be upheld throughout the legal process. Key rights include: 

Right to Representation 

Under Section 10(a) of the Charter, you have the right to legal representation upon arrest or detention. If this right is not upheld, the evidence collected during that time may be challenged in court. 

Right to be Informed 

In terms of Section 10(b), you must be informed of the reason for your arrest as soon as possible. This is crucial to ensuring that the legal process is conducted fairly. 

Right to Remain Silent 

Anything you say can be used against you in court. Itâ€™s important to exercise your right to remain silent and consult with a lawyer before making any statements to the police.
